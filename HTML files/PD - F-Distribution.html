The $F$-Distribution


$$ \begin{align} \text{Parameters:} \quad &d_1 > 0 \quad \text{type: degrees of freedom} \\ &d_2 > 0 \quad \text{type: degrees of freedom} \end{align} \\ $$ $$ \begin{align} \text{Support: }x \in [0,\infty) \end{align} \\~\\ $$ $$ \begin{align} \text{pdf: } f_X(x;d_1,d_2) & = \frac{\sqrt{\frac{(d_1x)^{d_1}d_2^{d_2}}{(d_1x+d_2)^{d_1+d_2}}}}{x\mathbf{B}\left ( \frac{d_1}{2},\frac{d_2}{2} \right )} \\ & = \frac{1}{x\mathbf{B}\left ( \frac{d_1}{2},\frac{d_2}{2} \right)}\left ( \frac{d_1}{d_2} \right )^{\frac{d_1}{2}} x^{\frac{d_1}{2}-1}\left ( 1 + \frac{d_1}{d_2}x \right )^{-\frac{d_1 + d_2}{2}} \\ \\ \text{where } \textbf{B} &\text{ is the beta function: } \textbf{B}(x,y) = \int_{0}{1} t^{x-1}(1-t)^{y-1}dt \\~\\~\\ \end{align}$$ $$ \begin{align} \text{cdf: } &F_X(x;d_1,d_2) = I_{\frac{d_1x}{d_1x+d_2}} \left ( \frac{d_1}{2},\frac{d_2}{2} \right ) \\ \\ \text{where } I &\text{ is the regularized incomplete beta function} \end{align} \\[1in] $$ $$ \\ \text{Mean: } E[X] = \frac{d_2}{d_2 - 2} \quad \text{for } d_2>2 \\ $$ $$ \text{Variance: } Var[X] = \frac{2d_2^2(d_1+d_2-2)}{d_1(d_2-2)^2(d_2-4)} \quad \text{for } d_2>4 \\ $$

The ratio of two independent chi-squared distributions, each scaled by the inverse of their respective degrees of freedom, inevitably follows what we call the F-Distribution

$$ \begin{align} \text{let }& \quad X_1 \sim \chi^2(x; d_1), \; X_2 \sim \chi^2(x; d_2) \\ \\ \text{then }& \quad Y = \frac{X_1/d_1}{X_2/d_2} \sim F(x;d_1,d_2) \end{align} $$

The $F$-distribution is primarily used in the context of $F$-tests. Recall that the following random variable follows a $\chi^2$ distribution with $n-1$ degrees of freedom :

$$ X = (n-1) \frac{s_0^2}{\sigma^2} \sim \chi^2(x;d=(n-1)) $$

Where $n$ is the sample size, $s_0^2$ is the sample variance, and $\sigma^2$ is the population variance. We can then express the ratio between two sample variances under the null-hypothesis in such a way that it inevitably follows an $F$-distribution

$$ \begin{align} X_1 &= (n_1-1) \frac{s_1^2}{\sigma_1^2} \sim \chi_1^2(x;d_1=(n_1-1)) \\ \\ X_2 &= (n_2-1) \frac{s_2^2}{\sigma_2^2} \sim \chi_2^2(x;d_2=(n_2-1)) \\ \\ \text{then} \quad Y &= \frac{(d_1 s_1^2/\sigma_1^2)/d_1}{(d_2 s_2^2/\sigma_2^2)/d_2} \\ \\ &= \frac{(s_1^2/\sigma_1^2)}{(s_2^2/\sigma_2^2)} \sim \textbf{F}(x;d1,d2) \end{align}$$

Under the null-hypothesis we assume that the population variances $\sigma_1^2,\sigma_2^2$ are equal. Therefore the simple ratio of sample variances ought to follow an $F$-distribution with the corresponding degrees of freedom $d_1,d_2$:

$$ \frac{s_1^2}{s_2^2} \sim \textbf{F}(x;d_1,d_2) $$

Therefore, since the above ratio follows a $F$-distribution (under the null-hypothesis), we have a statistically derived technique for gaining insight into the probabilistic relationships of statistics (i.e. sample variance or sample mean) between different samples. This method involves comparing the $F$-statistic (essentially the L.H.S. of the above expression) with the theorical null-hypothesis $F$-distribution (the R.H.S. of the above expression). We call any test of this form an $F$-test. In some cases, we may wish to determine whether two population variances are equal via the testing of sample variances (F-test of equality of variances). In other cases, we may wish to test whether population means are equal (ANOVA). The F-Distribution is the pivotal distribution that allows us to 'score' the $F$-value determined by the sample variances against the theoretical null-hypothesis F-distribution, and thus make probablistic statements about the relationship between the sample distribution and other entities (i.e. other samples or populations).

The F-Distribution is utilized in the following statistical tests:

Analysis of Variance (ANOVA)
F-test of the equality of two variances
Any test that involves the ratio of the sum of squares of i.i.d. standard normal random variables (i.e. $\chi^2$ rv's)
