Student's $t$-Distribution


$$ \begin{align} \text{Parameters:} \quad k > 0 \quad \text{type: degrees of freedom} \end{align} \\ $$ $$ \begin{align} \text{Support: }x \in (-\infty,+\infty) \end{align} \\~\\ $$ $$ \begin{align} \text{pdf: } f_X(x;k) & = \frac{\Gamma \left ( \frac{k+1}{2} \right )}{\sqrt{k\pi} \; \Gamma(k/2)} \left ( 1 + \frac{x^2}{k} \right )^{-\frac{v+1}{2}} \\~\\~\\ \end{align}$$ $$ \begin{align} \text{cdf: } \frac{1}{2} + x\Gamma \left ( \frac{k+1}{2} \right ) \times \frac{_2F_1 \left (\frac{1}{2}, \frac{k+1}{2}; \frac{3}{2}; -\frac{x^2}{k} \right )}{\sqrt{k \pi} \Gamma (k/2)} \\ \\ \text{where } _2F_1 \text{ is the hypergeometric function} \end{align} \\[1in] $$ $$ \\ \text{Mean: } E[X] = 0 \quad \text{for } k>1 \\ $$ $$ \begin{align} \text{Variance: } Var[X] = &\frac{k}{k-2} \quad \text{for } k>2 \\~\\ & \infty \text{ for } 1 < k \leq 2 \end{align} \\ $$

The ratio of a standard normal random variable $Z$ over the square-root of a chi-squared random variable $\chi_k^2$ (scaled by the inverse of its degrees of freedom $k$) inevitably follows a Student's $t$-distribution with $k$ degrees of freedom

$$ \begin{align} \text{let } \quad &X_1 \sim N(x; 0,1) \sim Z, \\ &X_2 \sim \chi^2(x; k) \\ \\ \text{then }& \quad Y = \frac{X_1}{\sqrt{X_2/k}} \sim t(x;k) \end{align} $$

Due to the fact that the $t$-distribution provides a model for the distribution of sample means as a function only dependent on sample size $n$ (referred to as $k = n-1$ degrees of freedom) -- importantly independent from the population mean and population variance -- the $t$-distribution is used in a number of ways in statistics.

In accordance with the Central Limit Theorem, given a sufficient sample size, the distribution of sample means follows a Normal distribution. Thus, if the population mean is known and the standard deviation is known or estimable, then we can utilize the Z-score test to determine the probability of whether or not the new sample ‘came from’ or ‘belongs to’ the parent population.

We do this by calculating a Z-score $z_X$ for the sample mean

$$ z_X = \frac{\bar{X} - \mu}{\sigma / \sqrt{n}}$$

where $\bar{X}$ = sample mean, $\mu$ = population mean, $\sigma$ = population standard deviation, and $n$ = sample size; denominator = [sample standard deviation]). The $z$-score can be interpreted as the distance (in units of standard deviation of the sample) between the population mean and the sample mean. Since the CLT tells us that the random variable $\frac{\bar{X} - \mu}{\sigma / \sqrt{n}}$ follows a standard normal distribution, then once we have calculated our $z$-score, we can easily reverse-compute insightful probabilites and confidence interverals regarding the relationship between the sample and population mean. Intuitively, the further the sample mean is from the population mean the lower the probability that the sample ‘came from’ the underlying population distribution. The greater the sample size $\rightarrow$ the smaller the standard deviation term $\rightarrow$ the greater the magnitude of the Z-score $\rightarrow$ the smaller the probability of obtaining that sample mean from the parent population.

However, the Z-score can only be utilized when the either sample size is sufficiently large or the population standard deviation is known. If neither of these conditions are true, then the Z-score does not provide a reliable account of the probabilistic relationship between the sample mean and the population mean. In such a case we turn to the Student’s t-test, which is another method of comparing a sample mean to the population mean that does not depend on population variance.

Whereas the random variable defined by

$$A = \frac{\bar{X} - \mu}{\sigma / \sqrt{n}}$$

Follows a standard normal distribution in virtue of the CLT, the random variable

$$B = \frac{\bar{X} - \mu}{s_n / \sqrt{n}}$$

Inevitably follows a $t$-distribution with $n-1$ degrees of freedom. Notice that the population standard deviation $\sigma$ has been replaced with sample standard deviation $s_n$. To see why this random variable follows a $t$-distribution, recall the characterization of the $t$-distribution as the ratio of a standard normal rv over the square-root of a chi-squared rv, scaled by the inverse of its degrees of freedom $k$:

$$ Y = \frac{Z}{\sqrt{\chi_k^2/k}} \sim t(x;k) $$

Also recall that the following random variable follows a $\chi^2$ distribution with $n-1$ degrees of freedom :

$$ C = (n-1) \frac{s_n^2}{\sigma^2} \sim \chi^2(x;k=(n-1)) $$

Where $n$ is the sample size, $s_n^2$ is the sample variance, and $\sigma^2$ is the population variance. Then, by making the appropriate substitutions, we can easily see that substituting sample standard deviation $s_n$ in place of population standard deviation $\sigma$ does indeed result in a $t$-distribution:

$$ \begin{align} A &= \frac{\bar{X} - \mu}{\sigma / \sqrt{n}} \sim N(0,1) \\ \\ C &= (n-1) \frac{s_n^2}{\sigma^2} \sim \chi^2(x;k=n-1) \\ \\ \text{then} \quad Y &= \frac{A}{\sqrt{C/k}}\\ \\ &= \frac{ \frac{\bar{X} - \mu}{\sigma / \sqrt{n}} }{ \sqrt{\frac{(n-1) \frac{s_n^2}{\sigma^2}}{(n-1)}} } \\~\\ &= \frac{ \frac{\bar{X} - \mu}{\sigma / \sqrt{n}} }{s_n/\sigma} \\~\\ &= \frac{ \bar{X} - \mu}{s_n/\sqrt{n}} \sim t(x;k=n-1) \end{align}$$

It is this formulation of the $t$-distribution that is crucial to statistics because it provides a theoretical reference-point through which we can compare any given sample mean to a pre-determined population mean. The $t$-statistic is calculated by $$t_X = \frac{ \bar{X} - \mu}{s_n/\sqrt{n}} $$ What is the probability of obtaining any given value of $t_X$? The $t$-distribution with $n-1$ degrees of freedom provides answers to the following questions:

What is the probability of obtaining any given value of $t_X$?
For what values of $t_X$ can I be greater than 95% certain that the sample differs from the population?
